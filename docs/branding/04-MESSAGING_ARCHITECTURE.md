## 5. Objection Handling Scripts

### Objection 1: "This sounds too good to be true"

**Response**:
"I appreciate the skepticism - it's warranted in AI.

That's exactly why we published proofs, not just claims.

Three steps to verify:
1. Read Theorem 5.1 (arXiv link)
2. Run our benchmarks (GitHub link)
3. Try the demo yourself (demo link)

Don't trust marketing. Verify mathematics."

---

### Objection 2: "Formal verification is too slow for agile development"

**Response**:
"Common misconception. Our benchmarks show 2.78Ã— *speedup*.

Why? Because mathematical foundations enable better optimization.
We know exactly what's safe to parallelize.

The real cost isn't verification time - it's debugging heuristic failures.
With proofs, you debug less, ship faster, with higher confidence."

---

### Objection 3: "We already use GitHub Copilot and it works fine"

**Response**:
"Copilot is excellent at what it does - code completion.

Miyabi handles different problems: multi-file tasks, complex workflows, end-to-end feature development.

Think of it this way:
- Copilot = better autocomplete
- Miyabi = autonomous development partner

Many teams use both. Copilot for line-level speed, Miyabi for task-level automation."

---

### Objection 4: "AI can't replace human developers"

**Response**:
"You're right. And we're not trying to.

Miyabi amplifies developers by handling verified patterns while humans focus on novel problems.

78% of development tasks are repetitive (CRUD, boilerplate, testing).
Miyabi handles those. With proofs.

22% is creative problem-solving, architecture, strategy.
That's what developers should focus on.

Amplification, not replacement."

---

### Objection 5: "Your research is too theoretical for our needs"

**Response**:
"Let me share practical results:

- 94.7% goal achievement on SWE-bench (real GitHub issues)
- 3 enterprise customers in production (NTT, KDDI, Rakuten)
- 2.78Ã— measured speedup in parallel execution

Theory enables practice. But we measure both.

Would a pilot program help demonstrate value in your environment?"

### Objection 6: "How do I know you'll succeed as a startup?"

**Response**:
"Fair question. Here's our foundation:

1. Research moat: Theorems can't be copied without equivalent research
2. Proven model: Sakana AI ($244M, $1.5B valuation) validated this path

## 9. Voice & Tone Guidelines by Message Type

### Announcing Research (Formal, Authoritative)

**Tone**: Academic rigor, precise language, humble about contribution

**Example**:
"We are pleased to announce the publication of 'The Law of Flickering Scenery' on arXiv. This work introduces the first formal convergence guarantee for autonomous agent systems (Theorem 5.1). We welcome peer review and community validation."

---

### Product Marketing (Confident, Clear)

**Tone**: Direct benefits, proof-backed claims, developer-friendly

**Example**:
"Miyabi reduces development costs by 78%. Not a promise. A measurement. Benchmarked on SWE-bench. Proven in production. Try it yourself: miyabi.dev"

---

### Competitive Positioning (Respectful, Factual)

**Tone**: Acknowledge competitors' strengths, differentiate on facts (not attacks)

**Example**:
"GitHub Copilot excels at code completion. Devin pioneered autonomous agents. Both are valuable tools. Miyabi differentiates through formal verification - the only system with mathematical convergence guarantees. Different problems, different solutions."

---

### Community Engagement (Warm, Inclusive)

**Tone**: Grateful, collaborative, learning-focused

**Example**:
"Thank you to everyone who's reviewed our SWML paper and contributed to the implementation. Open research means community validation. Your insights make the work stronger. Keep the feedback coming."
